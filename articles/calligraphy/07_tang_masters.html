The Tang Dynasty Masters: Yan Zhenqing and Liu Gongquan

The Tang dynasty (618-907 CE) represents the golden age of Chinese calligraphy, when the innovations of earlier periods were refined into models of perfection that would influence all subsequent practice. Among the many masters of this era, two figures stand out for their distinctive styles and enduring influence: Yan Zhenqing (709-785) and Liu Gongquan (778-865). Their works represent different paths to excellence—Yan's bold, muscular approach contrasting with Liu's elegant, disciplined precision—and together they established the standards against which all later Regular Script would be measured.

Yan Zhenqing: The Model of Loyalty and Strength

Liu Gongquan: The Pursuit of Perfect Structure

If Yan Zhenqing represents the expressive, emotional side of calligraphy, Liu Gongquan embodies its intellectual, structural dimension. Liu's style is characterized by razor-sharp precision, elegant proportions, and an almost mathematical clarity that has made his works the preferred models for beginners learning Regular Script.

Liu's famous statement "If the bone is strong, the flesh will follow" (gu li ze rou fu) summarizes his aesthetic philosophy. He believed that the underlying structure of characters was more important than surface effects, and his works demonstrate this principle with uncompromising clarity.

The characteristics of Liu's style include:

Sharp, angular strokes with clear beginnings and endings. Every movement of the brush is precise and intentional, leaving no ambiguity about how the stroke was formed.

Elongated structure creates characters that are taller than they are wide, with a sense of upward aspiration. Where Yan's characters sit solidly on the ground, Liu's seem to reach toward heaven.

Tight center, open periphery concentrates the main strokes toward the center of characters while allowing the extending strokes to fly outward. This creates dynamic tension within each character.

Consistent execution maintains the same high standard throughout long works. Liu's precision never falters, demonstrating the discipline that comes from decades of practice.

Liu's most celebrated works include the Xuan Mi Pagoda Stele (Xuan Mi Ta Bei), written when he was sixty-four, and the Shence Army Stele (Shence Jun Bei). Both demonstrate his mature style at its peak and have been standard models for students since the Tang dynasty.

The Shence Army Stele is particularly notable for its survival. Carved to commemorate a military victory, the stele was lost for centuries before being rediscovered in the twentieth century. Its preservation allows modern students to study Liu's work directly, rather than through copies and rubbings.

The Yan-Liu Comparison

Chinese calligraphy tradition has long recognized Yan Zhenqing and Liu Gongquan as complementary masters, representing different but equally valid approaches to the art. The conventional pairing "Yan's sinew and Liu's bone" (Yan jin Liu gu) captures their distinctive qualities—Yan's muscular, organic strength contrasting with Liu's sharp, structural precision.

For students, the choice between Yan and Liu as a model depends on temperament and purpose. Yan's style builds physical strength and emotional expressiveness; Liu's develops precision and intellectual clarity. Most serious students eventually study both, finding in their contrast a more complete understanding of what calligraphy can be.

The Qing dynasty theorist Kang Youwei argued that Liu's style, for all its excellence, represented a decline from the natural vitality of Yan's writing. Others have seen Liu as the necessary refinement of Yan's boldness, bringing discipline to expression. Both perspectives contain truth; the greatness of Tang calligraphy lies partly in the range of possibilities it established.

Historical Influence

The influence of Yan Zhenqing and Liu Gongquan on subsequent calligraphy cannot be overstated. During the Song dynasty, their works were collected and studied with passionate devotion. Emperor Huizong, himself a talented calligrapher, assembled the greatest collection of Tang masterpieces, including major works by both Yan and Liu.

The Ming and Qing dynasties saw repeated revivals of Yan and Liu study. Different periods favored one master over the other depending on aesthetic fashion, but both remained central to the tradition. The great Qing calligrapher He Shaoji combined Yan's boldness with Liu's structure to create a distinctive personal style that influenced modern practice.

In the twentieth century, both masters served as models for calligraphers seeking to preserve tradition amid revolutionary change. Yan Zhenqing's association with loyalty and moral integrity made him particularly appealing during periods of political upheaval; Liu Gongquan's precision offered a refuge of order in chaotic times.

Contemporary Relevance

Today, Yan Zhenqing and Liu Gongquan remain essential starting points for students of calligraphy. Their works are reproduced in countless textbooks, and their styles are taught in classrooms around the world. The clarity of their different approaches makes them ideal models for understanding the possibilities of Regular Script.

Contemporary artists continue to engage with their legacy. Some seek to recover the expressive power of Yan's writing in an age of technical reproduction; others push Liu's structural precision toward new extremes of abstraction. Both masters offer resources for innovation as well as tradition.

The contrast between Yan and Liu also speaks to broader questions about art and culture. Their example demonstrates that excellence can take different forms, that strength and refinement are both valid paths, that tradition contains multitudes. In a world that often demands conformity, Yan and Liu remind us that greatness can be achieved through different, even opposite, approaches.

Conclusion

Yan Zhenqing and Liu Gongquan represent the twin peaks of Tang dynasty calligraphy, establishing models of excellence that have guided practitioners for over a thousand years. Yan's bold, emotional writing and Liu's precise, structural approach offer complementary visions of what calligraphy can be, and the study of both provides a foundation for any serious engagement with the art.

Their works survive as more than historical documents. Every time a student copies the Draft of a Requiem to My Nephew or practices characters from the Xuan Mi Pagoda Stele, they enter into a living tradition, connecting with masters whose influence continues to shape Chinese culture. Yan and Liu are not merely figures from the past but present companions for anyone who takes up the brush.

In the end, the greatness of Yan Zhenqing and Liu Gongquan lies not in their technical perfection but in their humanity. Through their works, we encounter individuals of extraordinary character who found in calligraphy a way to express their deepest values. Their writing is a testament to the power of art to embody the best of human aspiration—a power that remains undiminished across the centuries.
